🌌 Aurora Activity
Geomagnetic Storm Level
Real-time planetary Kp index
G0
Kp —
—
The Kp index measures global geomagnetic activity on a scale of 0–9. Values above 5 indicate a geomagnetic storm — the higher the number, the further south the aurora can be seen.
27-Day Kp History
One full solar rotation
The sun rotates in about 27 days. Active regions that face Earth today may return to the same position in roughly 27 days — making historical data a useful predictor.

Hemispheric Power measures the total energy deposited into the polar regions by energetic particles. Higher values mean a more intense and widespread aurora.
Dst / SYM-H Index
Geomagnetic ring current intensity — nanoTeslas
—
nT
—
Storm level
The Dst index measures the strength of the geomagnetic ring current. Values near 0 = quiet. Below -50 nT = moderate storm. Below -100 nT = intense storm. The more negative, the more energy is stored in Earth's magnetosphere — and the more spectacular the aurora.
GOES Magnetometer
Hp · He · Hn components — 2-hour window (nT)
Hp — parallel He — equatorial Hn — northward
The GOES satellite measures Earth's magnetic field components. Rapid fluctuations — especially in Hp — indicate energy being injected into the magnetosphere. Sustained deflections during a storm are a strong real-time aurora indicator.
☀️ Solar Activity
Solar Wind — Speed
DSCOVR satellite · L1 point · 2-hour window
— km/s —
Solar wind speed drives how quickly disturbances reach Earth. Slow <350 km/s = quiet. Fast >600 km/s = elevated geomagnetic activity possible within hours of a southward Bz.
Solar Wind — Density
Proton density · p/cm³
— p/cm³ —
High density amplifies the pressure on Earth's magnetosphere. A sudden density spike combined with fast speed and southward Bz is a classic signature of an incoming CME.
Interplanetary Magnetic Field — Bz
North–south component · nT · 2-hour window
— nT —
Bz is the single most important aurora indicator. When negative (southward), it couples with Earth's northward field and pumps energy into the magnetosphere — directly fuelling aurora. The more negative, the stronger the effect.
Interplanetary Magnetic Field — Bt
Total field strength · nT · 2-hour window
— nT —
Bt is the total magnitude of the interplanetary magnetic field. A strong Bt (>10 nT) means more energy available — if Bz turns southward with a high Bt, significant geomagnetic activity often follows.

Solar flares are sudden bursts of radiation from the Sun's surface. Classes go A → B → C → M → X, each 10× stronger than the last. M and X class flares can disrupt radio communications and sometimes precede coronal mass ejections (CMEs).
GOES X-ray Flux
1–8 Å channel — 6-hour window
Primary GOES
M ≥10⁻⁵ C ≥10⁻⁶ B ≥10⁻⁷
X-ray flux from GOES measures solar flare intensity in real time. Sudden spikes indicate flares. The 1–8 Å channel is the standard used for the A/B/C/M/X classification. Watch for sharp rises followed by gradual decay — the classic flare signature.

Storm scale
Elevated proton flux indicates a solar radiation storm. S1–S5 scale: S1 is minor, S5 is extreme. During an S-class storm, high-latitude flights may be rerouted and HF radio can be disrupted.
